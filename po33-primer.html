01

Sampling

the sound

A sample is just a recorded sound. The craft is trimming it to the moment it hits, then pitching it to play. Pick a source, drag the handles to the start of the sound, then run it up the keys — that's melodic mode.

start ◀ 0%80% ▶ end

Melodic mode — one sample, across the keys

Trimmed tight = triggers crisp and on time.

The transient

The sharp spike at the front of a sound. Trim to just before it and your hit lands exactly on the beat.

Pitch = speed

Playing a sample faster raises its pitch, like spinning a record up. One sound becomes a melody.

02

Scales & keys

melody

Whether you're playing a synth or a pitched-up sample, you want notes from one scale, not random ones. Pick a root and a scale; the warm-lit keys are the "safe" notes that sound good together. Minor is the moody hip-hop default.

scale
root
type

Why it works

A scale is a fixed set of intervals. Stay inside it and melodies and basslines won't clash with the sample.

Pentatonic = easy

Five notes, no "wrong" ones. The fastest route to a melody that just works on the PO-33.

03

Punch-in FX

performance

Most samplers give you a bank of live effects you fire by holding a key while the beat runs — for quick variation, drops and fills (the PO-33 has 16). Press play, then press and hold a pad and let go.

a looping beat to mangle

Hold → effect on. Release → snaps back. That's punch-in.

04

Beat breakdowns

theory

Three College Dropout beats, same DNA: a soul vocal sped up over boom-bap drums. Tap load to set the device's tempo and drum skeleton, then hit play up top.

All Falls Down

~91 BPM · G♯ minor. Lauryn Hill's "Mystery of Iniquity," re-sung by Syleena Johnson after the sample was denied. Classic chipmunk-soul hook.

Through the Wire

~83 BPM. Chaka Khan's "Through the Fire" sped up — "Chaka on helium." Cut with Kanye's jaw wired shut after his crash.

Jesus Walks

~87 BPM. The ARC Choir's gospel "Walk With Me" over martial, marching-band drums. Straight and driving, no swing.

05

Song mode

arrangement

One 16-step bar loops forever — a song needs change. Chain patterns into an arrangement: tap A / B / C to add bars, then play to hear it move. Each block is one bar.

Typical shape

Verse (A) → hook (B) → verse → hook, with a fill (C) right before each change. That's most songs.

On hardware

On a groovebox or sampler you chain patterns in a song/write mode to build the full track — same idea as the blocks here.

06

Pocket glossary

Step

One slice of the bar — a 16th note. On / off decides if a sound fires there.

BPM

Beats per minute. Hip-hop ~85–95, house ~120–128. How fast the grid spins.

One-shot

Plays once per trigger — a kick, a clap, a stab. The opposite of a loop.

Loop

A sample trimmed to repeat seamlessly, like a drum break or held chord.

Pattern

One 16-step bar. Build several and you've got verse, fill, drop.

Chain

Linking patterns in order so a track moves instead of looping one bar.

07

Make your first track

  1. Sample short. Grab 4–6 sounds under a second — tap a cup, a clap, say a word. Keep them tight; sampler memory is always limited.
  2. Trim tight. Set each sample's start right on the transient. This single skill matters most.
  3. Lay the beat. Kick on 1·5·9·13, snare on 5·13, hats between — the pattern you built up top.
  4. Play a melody. Pitch a sample across the keys like a synth and sequence a bassline, in a single scale, under the drums.
  5. Arrange it. Make a second pattern, chain them, add punch-in FX. That's a track.

These five moves are the whole craft — they transfer to any sampler, groovebox, or DAW. The instrument above just lets you rehearse them.
