How a PO-style sampler works: the numbered keys change meaning depending on the function you hold. Hold sound + 1-16 to choose a slot, hold record + 1-16 to sample into one, press write to enter step-write mode, then press 1-16 to place or remove hits.

↓ now: sampling ↓

01

Sampling

the sound

A sample is just a recorded sound. The craft is trimming it to the moment it hits, then pitching it to play. Pick a source, drag the handles to the start of the sound, then run it up the keys — that's melodic mode.

start ◀ 0%80% ▶ end

Melodic mode — one sample, across the keys

Trimmed tight = triggers crisp and on time.

The transient

The sharp spike at the front of a sound. Trim to just before it and your hit lands exactly on the beat.

Pitch = speed

Playing a sample faster raises its pitch, like spinning a record up. One sound becomes a melody.

Practice here

Record a clap, word, desk tap, or use blip/bass. Trim it until the trigger feels immediate, then pitch it up and down.

On PO-33

Hold record + 1-8 for melodic slots or record + 9-16 for drum slots. Line-in records instead of the mic when a cable is plugged in.

02

Scales & keys

melody

Whether you're playing a synth or a pitched-up sample, you want notes from one scale, not random ones. Pick a root and a scale; the warm-lit keys are the "safe" notes that sound good together. Minor is the moody hip-hop default.

scale
root
type

Why it works

A scale is a fixed set of intervals. Stay inside it and melodies and basslines won't clash with the sample.

Pentatonic = easy

Five notes, no "wrong" ones. The fastest route to a melody that just works on the PO-33.

Practice here

Pick minor pentatonic, play only three or four notes, then repeat the phrase until it feels like a hook.

On PO-33

Record into slots 1-8 when you want one sound stretched across the keys. In drum slots 9-16, the keys trigger slices instead.

03

Punch-in FX

performance

Most samplers give you a bank of live effects you fire by holding a key while the beat runs — for quick variation, drops and fills. Press play, then press and hold a pad and let go.

a looping beat to mangle

Hold → effect on. Release → snaps back. On PO-33, hold FX + 1-15; FX + 16 clears saved effects when write is on.

04

Beat breakdowns

theory

A beat starts as a skeleton, not a finished song. Put kick where the head nods, snare on the backbeat, hats between the pulses, then let a sample or bassline answer the drums. Tap load to hear three different drum feels.

Boom-bap

~91 BPM. Kick on 1 plus one off-beat, snare on 5 and 13, hats on eighth notes. Good under a chopped vocal or piano stab.

Laid-back

~83 BPM. Slower tempo and more swing. Keep the sample sparse so the groove has room to lean.

Marching

~87 BPM. Straight, driving drums with extra snare movement near the end of the bar. Useful for a fill before a change.

On PO-33

Select a drum sound with sound + 9-16, press write, then tap steps 1-16. Active steps light up; tap lit steps again to remove them.

05

Song mode

arrangement

One 16-step bar loops forever — a song needs change. Chain patterns into an arrangement: tap A / B / C to add bars, then play to hear it move. Each block is one bar.

Typical shape

Verse (A) → hook (B) → verse → hook, with a fill (C) right before each change. That's most songs.

On PO-33

Hold pattern and press the pattern numbers in order to chain them. You can repeat a pattern, like 1, 1, 1, 4.

06

Pocket glossary

Step

One slice of the bar — a 16th note. On / off decides if a sound fires there.

BPM

Beats per minute. Hip-hop ~85–95, house ~120–128. How fast the grid spins.

One-shot

Plays once per trigger — a kick, a clap, a stab. The opposite of a loop.

Loop

A sample trimmed to repeat seamlessly, like a drum break or held chord.

Pattern

One 16-step bar. Build several and you've got verse, fill, drop.

Chain

Linking patterns in order so a track moves instead of looping one bar.

Melodic slot

PO-33 slots 1-8 play one recorded sound across the keys, useful for bass, chords, vocals and hooks.

Drum slot

PO-33 slots 9-16 slice the recording so each key triggers a different piece of the sound.
